# Google — Hiring Culture & CV Tips

## What Google Looks For
Google hires on four core attributes:
1. **General cognitive ability** — structured problem solving, data-driven thinking
2. **Leadership** — leading through influence, not authority; can be demonstrated at any level
3. **Googleyness** — comfort with ambiguity, collaboration, intellectual humility
4. **Role-related knowledge** — technical depth for engineering; product sense for PM

## CV Tips for Google Applications

### For Software Engineers
- Google values system design depth — show evidence of designing systems at scale
- Open source contributions signal genuine passion
- Mention specific Google technologies if you've used them: Go, Kubernetes, Borg, Spanner (academic context is fine)
- Competitive programming or algorithmic problem solving (Codeforces, LeetCode, IOI) is highly regarded
- Google's SWE ladder: L3 (new grad) → L4 → L5 (senior) → L6 (staff) → L7 (principal)

### For Product Managers
- Google APM programme is highly competitive — emphasise analytical rigour
- Show comfort with ambiguity and 0-to-1 product work
- Data-driven decision making must be evident throughout
- Collaboration across engineering, UX, and business is expected at all levels

### Keywords That Resonate at Google
distributed systems, scalability, reliability, site reliability engineering, SRE, machine learning, 
TensorFlow, Go, Kubernetes, data pipelines, A/B testing, user research, growth, monetisation,
cross-functional, ambiguity, first principles, impact at scale

## ATS Notes
Google uses a proprietary ATS. CVs are also reviewed by technical sourcing recruiters. 
Clarity and structure matter — Google recruiters review hundreds of CVs daily.
Keep to 1-2 pages. Use bullet points, not paragraphs.

## Common Rejection Reasons
- Bullets describe tasks, not impact — Google cares deeply about measurable outcomes
- No evidence of working at scale (millions of users, high QPS, large datasets)
- Generic leadership claims without specific examples
- Poor structure or formatting — signals lack of attention to detail
